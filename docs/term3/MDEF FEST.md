## ★ Abstract[¶](#abstract "Permanent link")

**Find Your Match at Elisava.**

![](../images/find-your-match-installation.jpg)

Participants encounter QR codes around campus and are drawn in by the oldest social instinct: the desire to connect. They complete what feels like a personality-based dating form, answering 15 questions about their health, lifestyle, emotional patterns, political behaviour, and relationship style. What they are not told is that every answer is secretly mapped to one of seven real institutional data buyer categories and scored against documented market rates for personal data. At the reveal event, each participant finds a folder bearing their anonymous ID and a euro value: the price their data would fetch on the open market.

> "You came looking for a date. We found 7 buyers for your data."

## ★ Concept & Research[¶](#concept-research "Permanent link")

**From Biometric Stock Market to Find Your Match.**

This project is the middle act of a three-part research arc. Mochi (2024) explored data, the body, and ownership. Safe Hands will examine data as post-mortem inheritance. Find Your Match sits at the moment of extraction — when personal data leaves the body and enters the market.

The direct predecessor is the Biometric Stock Market (2024/25), which mapped six biometric data types to four institutional buyers and traced seven feedback loops that compound into a complete digital twin. The key finding: data types are not equal. Health and behavioral intimacy commands the highest market rates. Basic demographics sit at the bottom. This hierarchy became the valuation engine for Find Your Match.

**The Conceptual Trap.**

The shift from the Biometric Stock Market to this project is a shift in stakes. In the earlier piece, the shock came from seeing friends' data commodified. In this version, the shock is personal: the participant IS the data point. They did not know they signed up for this. They thought they were looking for love. The gap between what they came for and what they found is the work.

**The Conceptual Inversion.**

The filler questions are weighted 3× in the matching algorithm — not because they are more useful to buyers, but because they are completely useless to buyers. The questions that data markets cannot monetize are the ones that matter most for human connection. This asymmetry is a conceptual statement built into the code.
